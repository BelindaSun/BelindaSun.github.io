The Great Golf Hoe Adventure

Today Liudodo took me to Topgolf in San Jose. Technically it’s a golf entertainment venue — part high-tech driving range, part restaurant, part social playground. In other words: exactly the kind of place where dignity goes to die and laughter thrives.

It instantly reminded me of the time years ago when I went “fun bowling” with colleagues at headquarters. We had a list of ridiculous challenge moves to follow — the more dramatic, awkward, and unhinged, the better. We laughed so hard our stomachs hurt. To this day, I still smile thinking about it.

There was also that unforgettable snooker session: a group of people who barely knew how to play enthusiastically producing every possible form of chaos, disaster, and accidental comedy. Absolute cinematic nonsense. Tremendous fun.

Today’s golf outing, thankfully, did not disappoint either.

In fact, it became a glorious remake of all previous sporting catastrophes.

At first, beginner’s luck struck again.

(Years ago at headquarters bowling, I once opened with a perfect strike and shocked my coworkers into wild cheering. “Don’t get excited,” I told them calmly. “Clearly a performance malfunction.”)

Today started similarly. My first dozen or so golf shots were surprisingly decent. The system even flashed a message on-screen:

“Belinda, you are looking bright.”

Well then.

Apparently talent simply cannot remain hidden forever.

Naturally, I became a little pleased with myself.

“I should go bigger,” I declared confidently.

So I selected the Driver — the longest club — and began doing a few extremely professional-looking practice motions.

Unfortunately, during one of these elegant warmups, I accidentally knocked the ball off the tee. 😆

No matter.

I reset the ball, took another poised practice swing, then delivered what I believed was an impressively athletic full-power strike.

I missed the ball completely.

The ball remained peacefully where it had always been.

At this point I was already laughing too hard to function.

After recovering, I tried again.

Another mighty professional swing. 🏌️

THUD.

This time I violently attacked the turf mat instead. The ball, once again, remained entirely untouched.

Liudodo was laughing uncontrollably.

“How are you even doing that?” she asked.

“I honestly have no idea,” I replied. 🤷‍♀️

Then she informed me I was close to damaging the facility’s flooring. 🤣

Still undeterred, I continued.

The next ball rolled weakly sideways onto the ground beside me.

The next one technically made it onto the field… though mostly through what appeared to be gravitational surrender rather than athletic achievement. 😆

In summary: the more I played, the less capable I became.

A truly fascinating phenomenon.

Liudodo finally diagnosed the issue:

“I think you’re treating the golf club like a farming hoe. Your motion looks like you’re plowing land.”

Impossible, I thought.

Then I watched the replay video.

She was absolutely right.

Energetic? Yes.

Elegant? Not remotely.

I was operating entirely on brute force.

At one point, someone in the neighboring bay was holding up a phone, and I honestly couldn’t tell whether he was on a video call or secretly recording me for a future comedy compilation titled Golf Fails of Northern California. 🤣

Later I sighed dramatically:

“This learning curve is getting a little long.”

Liudodo comforted me wisely:

“Every professional career eventually encounters bottlenecks.”

Toward the end of the first round, my balls ran out. I shook the dispenser with the club and nothing came out.

I immediately asked:

“They didn’t stop giving me balls because I’m too terrible, right?” 🤣

Then came the turning point of my athletic career.

A man who looked genuinely skilled happened to walk past our bay on his way out. He paused, watched me for a moment — probably out of humanitarian concern — and demonstrated a proper grip technique.

(I suspect he simply could not watch any longer. 🤣)

I copied his grip.

First shot: points.

Second shot: 4 points.

Third shot: 14 points.

A personal best!

Suddenly I was unstoppable.

Clearly this thousand-mile horse merely needed the guidance of a 伯乐. 🤣

The entire two hours were pure joy.

We ate, drank, laughed, humiliated ourselves athletically, and finally headed home.

As we walked downstairs afterward, I rubbed my legs and said:

“My legs feel a little sore now.”

Liudodo nodded sympathetically.

“Well, farming the land isn’t exactly light labor.”

🤣

Later that evening we drove to the Apple Store at Westfield to pick up my iPad, then sat beneath the shade of roadside trees eating ice cream and chatting.

The sunlight was golden.

The temperature was perfect.

The scenery was gentle and beautiful.

One of those effortless afternoons where time itself feels soft around the edges.

A perfect, peaceful mother-daughter day.

The kind you never quite want to end.
