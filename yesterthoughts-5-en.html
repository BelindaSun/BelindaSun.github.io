A Journey Through Van Gogh’s Netherlands

The theme of this trip to the Netherlands was Van Gogh.

Although Van Gogh’s original works can be found in museums in New York, San Francisco, Paris, Tokyo, and other major cities, the two destinations I visited over these days — the Van Gogh Museum in Amsterdam and the Kröller-Müller Museum in De Hoge Veluwe National Park — together hold the world’s largest and second-largest collections of his works. It was something I had long looked forward to.

Before arriving, I had also planned to visit the Anne Frank House, but it required booking tickets more than forty days in advance. I missed it — just as I had missed the Van Gogh Museum the previous year due to the same lack of planning. Perhaps it is better this way; it leaves a reason to return. Next time, I can also visit Rembrandt’s house. In the end, however, time proved to be far too limited.

The Rijksmuseum sits right next to the Van Gogh Museum, and I found myself thinking that it might even be more rewarding in some ways. Still, I spent hours in Van Gogh’s world, wearing the audio guide, moving slowly from one painting to another, listening carefully, and feeling a deep ache of sympathy for his life.

At one point, I even searched for Starry Night on my headphones and nearly cried 🎧.

“He was born. He painted. He died.
A golden wheat field.
A flock of crows crying across the sky.”

A life so brief, yet so intensely luminous — and painfully heartbreaking.

In truth, I barely had time to listen to Starry Night. I was too absorbed in the explanations of his works, many of which I replayed twice. It reminded me of childhood days listening to radio programs about European painting, music, and sculpture, and of university courses in Western art history where I would sit completely absorbed in the stories behind each masterpiece.

Somewhere along that path, a quiet clarity emerged about a question I had long been circling — what I might want to do after retirement.

Perhaps I could spend time in university libraries, or wander through museums, lingering for hours in front of a single book or painting. A rhythm of one book or one artwork per week would hardly leave room for boredom or emptiness. (And the engineer in the family would probably approve — no disruption, no sudden interest in square dancing or mahjong, both of which he considers questionable hobbies [mischievous grin]).

At the Rijksmuseum, I purchased an audio tour and began exploring, quickly realizing that it was impossible to see everything. I had to move at a glance, but I did not miss the highlights. Every painting with a commentary, I listened to carefully.

The Night Watch drew the largest crowd — naturally, as the museum’s crown jewel. A security guard stood nearby, quietly watching over it. As closing time approached, I was gently ushered out, still unwilling to leave. In the final minutes before the doors closed, two visitors even ran in just to catch a glimpse of the painting.

Outside, the contrast was striking. On the grass, people were enjoying their weekend with unhurried ease. A man played the saxophone as if time itself had no urgency. An elderly gentleman was deeply focused on a giant chessboard. Pigeons splashed in the canal. A small red car sat parked by the road, almost toy-like in its cuteness 🚗.

It all felt unbearably peaceful — the kind of life where one could simply decide to visit a museum at any moment, without urgency, without barriers 😌.

The next day I visited the Kröller-Müller Museum in the forest park, which also holds over two hundred Van Gogh works along with pieces by other masters. At the entrance, a tall, slender sculpture immediately reminded me of similar works outside office buildings in Beijing — perhaps inspired by the same modernist lineage [grin].

Later that evening, I traveled to The Hague. Although I did not manage to see Girl with a Pearl Earring, I did visit the lively beach, and took a tram for the first time to see the city from a different perspective. After wandering through a shopping district, I took the train back to Delft.

On the walk from the station to the hotel, the evening light was falling — though the sky remained bright, as it does in northern summers where darkness arrives only after nine.

Delft was breathtaking. True canals, true bridges, true quiet beauty. On weekends, the entire town seemed to come alive. When the weather is good, every day feels like a holiday — and this was a bank holiday Monday.

When I visited last year, it was early spring, still cold, but full of blooming tulips. This time, the scenery was different — late spring meeting early summer — equally gentle, equally comforting.

The painter Vermeer was born here, though none of his works remain in Delft today. Still, I saw The Little Street at the Rijksmuseum, depicting this very town. The moment I stood before it, I felt an unexpected wave of emotion.

“In The Little Street, time is completely suspended. The women washing, sewing, and playing remain forever still. Within the painting, time quietly flows past our feet.”

“More than 360 years have passed. The people and the streets are long gone. Yet the painting still quietly tells their story.”

That, too, was what this journey gave me — a sense of suspended time, and a feeling that lingers long after departure.

Something to return to. Something to continue.

— 19 May 2024
