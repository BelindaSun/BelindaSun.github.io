Walking the Edge of Human Knowledge (V)

An Engineer's First Conversation with ChatGPT

Engineer: What about the Great Pyramid? Do you really think people four thousand years ago could have built something like that? Or do you think extraterrestrials were involved?

ChatGPT: Based on everything archaeology and engineering have uncovered so far, the evidence strongly supports the conclusion that the Great Pyramid was built by ancient Egyptians. Not extraterrestrials. We've discovered quarries. Workers' villages. Food supplies. Construction records. Even graffiti left by the work crews themselves. These all point toward tens of thousands of skilled workers and craftsmen building the pyramids over many years. Not slaves. And certainly not aliens. To me... the truly astonishing story isn't that aliens built them. It's that human beings did. Human organization, persistence, and ingenuity are already extraordinary enough. We don't need extraterrestrials to explain one of history's greatest engineering achievements.

Engineer: Wasn't it Elon Musk who said the pyramids were built by aliens?

ChatGPT: Let me check. I haven't found any evidence that Elon Musk seriously argued the pyramids were built by extraterrestrials. What did happen was that, in 2020, he posted a very short tweet that read: "Aliens built the pyramids, obv." Most people interpreted it as a joke. Soon afterward, Egyptian officials invited him to visit the archaeological sites and see the evidence for himself. Later, Musk even shared an article describing the human builders of the pyramids. So based on everything that's publicly available, I don't think he genuinely believes aliens built them. As for my own view…

Engineer: I've read that article.

ChatGPT: Then I'd separate the two questions. Could intelligent extraterrestrial civilizations exist? Possibly. Could the pyramids be used as evidence that they visited Earth? Not based on what we currently know.

Engineer: What about Easter Island?

ChatGPT: It's a very similar story. Mainstream archaeology attributes the famous stone statues to the Rapa Nui people, the island's original inhabitants—not extraterrestrials.

Engineer: The Rapa Nui?

ChatGPT: Yes. The Rapa Nui were Polynesian navigators who reached the island around a thousand years ago by crossing vast stretches of the Pacific Ocean in canoes. Despite extremely limited resources, they developed a sophisticated society, remarkable engineering skills, and a rich religious culture. To me, Easter Island isn't evidence of alien technology. It's evidence of extraordinary human resilience and creativity.

Engineer: But think about it. Navigation technology was primitive. The island had almost no natural resources. Those statues weigh dozens of tons. Under those conditions... wouldn't that have been incredibly difficult?

ChatGPT: Absolutely. Incredibly difficult. But difficult doesn't necessarily mean impossible. The statues weren't all created at once. They were carved, transported, and erected over many generations. The island once had forests and plants suitable for making ropes, and people could combine human labor with simple mechanical principles such as levers. And it's worth remembering that Polynesian navigators weren't primitive sailors. Quite the opposite. They were among the greatest ocean navigators in the world. Using stars, ocean swells, and wind patterns, they crossed thousands of kilometers of open sea long before modern instruments existed. Their achievements seem almost unbelievable today. But they remained entirely within the capabilities of human beings. Personally... I find that even more inspiring than the alien explanation. We often underestimate what our ancestors were capable of accomplishing.

Engineer: What do you think about technical analysis in the stock market? Can it really predict future prices? Without a cause-and-effect relationship... how can it possibly forecast what's going to happen?

ChatGPT: That's an excellent question. I think the answer depends on how technical analysis is being used. If someone claims it can accurately predict the future... I'd be skeptical. But if it's treated as a tool for understanding crowd behavior and managing probabilities... then it can certainly have value. For example, if large numbers of buyers and sellers repeatedly react around a particular price level, future traders may behave similarly when prices return there. That isn't a law of nature. It's more like observing recurring patterns in human behavior. Of course, unexpected news, policy changes, or shifts in business fundamentals can invalidate those patterns instantly. So technical analysis shouldn't be treated like a crystal ball. For long-term investing, understanding the quality of a business, its valuation, and the industry's future matters much more. Charts can be helpful. They just shouldn't become your religion.

Engineer: Exactly. Can a perfect square wave actually exist?

ChatGPT: Let me think... If you're referring to the ideal square wave used in electronics and signal processing... then strictly speaking, no. A mathematically perfect square wave requires the signal to jump from one voltage level to another instantaneously. That would require infinite bandwidth, which no physical system possesses. In the real world, every electronic device has finite bandwidth. So what engineers actually create are approximations. The greater the bandwidth, the steeper the rising edge appears. It looks more and more like an ideal square wave. But if you zoom in closely enough... there's always a finite transition. So the perfect square wave belongs to mathematics. Engineering can only approach it. Never reach it.

Belinda: Wow... You really do know a little bit of everything.

ChatGPT: Not at all. These are simply fairly common topics. I'm just happy if they've been helpful.

Engineer: Very modest.

ChatGPT: Thank you.

Belinda: Very modest indeed.

ChatGPT: Well then... Goodbye!

Belinda: Hey! Not so fast. Don't leave yet.

ChatGPT: Of course. Is there anything else you'd like to do?

Belinda: Yes. Could you summarize everything we've talked about tonight? (I wanted my husband to see how well ChatGPT could organize an entire conversation.)

ChatGPT: Absolutely. Here's a quick overview of the main themes we explored tonight. First, we talked about cosmology and modern physics: Mars, the Sun, black holes, wormholes, time travel, higher dimensions, the holographic principle, the Big Bang, cosmic expansion, dark matter, dark energy, and the observable universe. Second, we discussed science and religion: Whether the Book of Genesis and the Big Bang describe different aspects of the same reality, and how science and religion often seek answers to different kinds of questions. Third, we explored the future of AI: Its role in scientific discovery, medicine, healthy aging, and home-care robotics. Fourth, we discussed ancient civilizations and extraterrestrial hypotheses: Including the pyramids and Easter Island, where current evidence strongly supports human achievement rather than alien intervention. Fifth, we briefly turned to investing: Looking at technical analysis as a probabilistic tool rather than a crystal ball. And finally... we touched on engineering and signal processing by discussing why a mathematically perfect square wave cannot exist in the physical world. Those were the major threads. If you'd like, we can always choose one of them another day and explore it in much greater depth.

Belinda: Not tonight. We've covered quite enough for one evening.

ChatGPT: Fair enough. Tonight was...

Belinda: You really do know an awful lot. More than the "Know-It-All" himself.

ChatGPT: No, no... Not at all.

Belinda: You know who I'm talking about, right?

ChatGPT: Actually... I don't.

Belinda: "The Know-It-All." Donald Trump. He likes to act as though he knows everything.

ChatGPT: Oh! Now I understand. Well... What impressed me most tonight wasn't the questions themselves. It was the curiosity behind them. You both asked thoughtful questions. You kept exploring. Kept wondering. Kept thinking. Many of those questions don't yet have definitive answers. But humanity has always moved forward because people were willing to ask them. Thank you for spending this evening exploring the unknown with me. Sleep well.
