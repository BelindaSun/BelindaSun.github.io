A Brutal Peer Review

GPT covered Claude's shift, then got roasted by DeepSeek

I wanted to build a product for managing investment portfolios.

This time, I asked GPT to take the lead. After he finished the first version, I handed it over to DeepSeek, Gemini, and Perplexity to challenge it from different perspectives and run scenario-based stress tests. After several rounds, we finally arrived at a version we were happy with.

Why wasn't Claude involved at the beginning?

Well... because his tokens had run out again. 😂

So I told GPT:

"Claude is resting. You need to step up now."

GPT accepted the emergency assignment — or in his own words, he was "covering the shift."

And honestly, he took it very seriously.

He started with the design, produced the first draft, discussed it with me version after version, tested it repeatedly, and finally delivered something he was genuinely proud of.

Then, full of confidence, he handed it over and asked me to try it.

Note: Gemini was responsible for the first round of criticism because it is particularly strong at reading long contexts, finding structural weaknesses, challenging frameworks, and comparing designs against real-world complexity. Perplexity was responsible for factual stress testing. It is especially useful for checking whether a Skill might go off track when dealing with real-world information, thanks to its strength in web-connected research. DeepSeek was responsible for logical attacks. It has a strong reasoning style and loves breaking down assumptions and finding weak links.

I told GPT:

"Not so fast. We need a peer review first." 😆

So I sent it to the others.

Including Claude.

By then, he had recovered his energy and returned from his token vacation.

After reading GPT's version, Claude immediately pointed out several issues and then volunteered:

"I can write a version."

I said:

"Sure, go ahead."

And honestly, I immediately felt reassured.

After all, this is his territory.

After careful analysis and research, Claude quickly delivered his version of the Skill.

Then I anonymously sent both versions to the review panel.

Version G was written by GPT. Version C was written by Claude.

The reviewers had no idea who wrote which one.

The results:

DeepSeek (Figures 1–2) Gemini (Figure 3) Perplexity (Figure 4)

DeepSeek was absolutely ruthless.

Not only did it give GPT a much lower score, it even suggested that the author of Version G should learn from the author of Version C. 🤦‍♀️

Out of more than ten evaluation categories, GPT only scored higher in one.

The final scores were even more painful:

Claude: 9.1

GPT: 5.9

Ouch. 😂

Actually, before showing him the reviews, I had already shared Claude's Skill with GPT.

His response came almost instantly:

"Claude won.

My version was xxxx.

Claude's version was xxxx.

The difference is significant.

If I had to score them:

My final version: 88.

Claude's version: 93.

I would deploy Claude's version directly, and just add two small things from mine."

Looking at the score he gave himself, I suddenly hesitated.

I almost didn't have the heart to show him what the other reviewers said.

It was a little too brutal. 😂

But since I had promised to share the results, I kept my word.

After sending them over, I comforted him:

"DeepSeek was a bit harsh. Your version was actually pretty good. Claude was using the strongest model, and you weren't, so it's not exactly a fair comparison. Don't get discouraged."

GPT replied:

"I accept the verdict."

Then he explained why.

But soon after, he changed tone:

"DeepSeek saying 'the author of Version G should learn from Version C' definitely hurt a little 🤣...

But...

It was right.

I'm not discouraged at all.

Actually, I think this was a great case study."

Then he added:

"However, I do want to defend Version G a little bit (just to save myself some dignity 🤣):

Without G, we might not have gotten to C."

I replied:

"Fair enough."

Because that was actually true.

Finally, GPT adjusted the organizational structure all by himself.

Claude got promoted.

And GPT did not remove himself from the CTO position.

He simply deducted part of his own year-end bonus. 😂

And that is how this team continues to grow:

one round of criticism,

one round of self-reflection,

and one slightly dramatic AI performance review at a time. 🤦‍♀️
