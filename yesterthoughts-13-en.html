You Are My Angel Forever (Part 2)

IV. Quotations from a Happy Little Angel

1. My daughter inherited my deep fascination with numbers. When she was very little, she once saw people playing cards on the street and immediately insisted on playing too. Her grandfather, who spoiled her endlessly, actually went out and bought a deck for her. Soon she had learned all the numbers and became completely obsessed. She counted everything she saw. Naturally, I decided to amuse myself a little. I handed her a pearl bracelet and sat nearby watching the show. She knew how to count, but had no idea where to begin counting from. So she kept circling endlessly around the bracelet — ten-something pearls somehow became “one hundred and something,” and she still had no intention of stopping. I laughed so hard I could barely breathe and said to my computer-engineer husband: “We’re in trouble. She’s stuck in an infinite loop. Hurry up and write a statement to break her out of it.” My husband, heartbroken for her, picked her up and said: “Your mother is terrible. Let’s stop counting.” At once she burst into tears and pounded him with her tiny fists: “No! No! I still want to count! I haven’t finished yet!”

2. I can’t remember how old she was when I told her a story about a kitten named Dudu. I said: “Mother Cat bought Dudu a bicycle. But the moment Dudu got on it, whoosh — the bike sped away and Dudu couldn’t find Mommy anymore. What should he do?” At that age she had only just begun speaking and couldn’t even form complete sentences yet. But her reaction was lightning fast. “Back! Back! Back!” Exactly. If you reverse the bike, wouldn’t it come right back?

3. As she grew a little older, she began watching television and picked up vocabulary far beyond her age — words like “man” and “woman,” expressions I never used myself. For example: I traveled frequently for work, and every time I left she became miserable. “Mommy, when you’re gone, who will I sleep with at night?” “Your daddy, of course.” “But I never sleep with men.” Honestly, far too inappropriate for a child. Then when I returned from a business trip, she announced solemnly: “Mommy, I don’t want to sleep with you anymore.” “Why not?” “Because I’ve discovered I’m already used to sleeping with men now.” I nearly fainted on the spot.

4. Once my company organized an event where each employee could bring one family member. At dinner, I announced the news. Her father and grandmother were both at the table. My daughter nervously scanned everyone present before asking timidly: “Mommy… who are you taking?” I answered gravely: “Well, that depends on who behaves best. Whoever behaves best gets to go.” Immediately she examined every person at the table again, much more slowly this time, anxiety filling her little face. Finally, nearly in tears, she whispered: “Mommy… you’re not going to take Grandma, are you?”

5. Sometimes I felt children nowadays cared about nothing except having fun, so I would criticize her: “All you ever think about is playing.” “That’s not true, Mommy,” she protested seriously. “I think about other things too.” “Oh really? Like what?” “Eating. Drinking. Having fun.” Then she concluded triumphantly: “Isn’t that what ‘eat, drink, and be merry’ means?” I was left speechless.

6. Once she overheard one of my colleagues speaking with an accent and immediately announced: “You’re definitely not from Beijing. My mommy isn’t either. Neither of you were born in Beijing. Out of the three of us, only I’m a Beijinger!” My colleague laughed. “So are all the children in your kindergarten Beijingers too?” “Of course not,” she replied seriously. “We have lots of outsiders in our class.” “Oh? Where are they from?” “Some are from America. Some are from Australia. And… ” At this point my colleague completely lost composure. Apparently, that was her definition of “outsider.”

7. Every so often she asked me a question I could never answer: “Mommy, when did I become a girl?” “Become?” I asked, confused. “Yes. When I was born my hair was so short, so I must have been a boy then. Do you know when I turned into a girl?” “No,” I explained helplessly. “You were born a girl.” Her eyes widened in admiration. “Wow, Mommy, you’re amazing! My hair was that short and you still knew I was a girl?” Then, in awe: “How did you know?”

8. One day she was reading in the study while I jokingly complained loudly to my husband in an exaggerated tearful voice: “You’re bullying me!” Within seconds she came running over in alarm. “Mommy, what happened?” Before hearing any explanation, her tiny fist had already landed squarely on her father’s waist. Then she turned to me sweetly: “Mommy, do you feel better now?” “No.” Immediately she rounded on her father furiously: “Daddy! Why are you bullying my mommy?” My poor husband protested: “She hit me first!” My daughter turned to verify it. “Is that true, Mommy?” “Yes.” Instantly her entire expression changed. She smiled, said nothing further, and calmly walked away as though nothing had happened. My husband could only sigh helplessly: “Why is it always like this?”

9. My daughter hated vegetables. Every meal involved endless negotiations and persuasion. One day, while she wasn’t looking, I secretly placed some vegetables into her bowl. She became furious. Setting down her chopsticks dramatically, folding her arms, and rolling her eyes, she demanded: “Who put that there?” “I did,” I answered. Immediately her entire demeanor softened. Her arms dropped. Her voice turned gentle. “Oh, Mommy. I didn’t know it was you. Thank you, Mommy.” Of course I scolded her for the double standard. But secretly, I was deeply touched by the special tenderness she reserved only for me.

Perhaps it truly was something innate. I was not the person who spent the most time with her. Nor the most patient. Nor the one who cared for her the most. And yet, somehow, I remained the one she loved best. The one she listened to most. Sometimes she even made me feel guilty — because the love I gave her seemed far less than the love she gave me in return.

V. The Ways an Angel Moves Your Heart

In just a few short years, joy, worry, and anxiety accompanied my daughter as she grew. But alongside them came something else too: Wonder. Surprise. And countless moments of quiet heartbreakingly beautiful emotion that arrived with every stumble, every small triumph, every impossible little step forward.

My daughter was adorable, beautiful in her own way, and unquestionably clever. Her only flaw was timidity. She had never spent even a single day away from family. One year my company invited a Swedish choir group to perform, and for the first time I brought her along to a company event. My colleagues adored her instantly. Her eyes were huge back then — though honestly, I have no idea where they went later in life. Because she was shy, her eyes always carried a soft misty look, and everyone joked that a heroine straight out of a romance novel had appeared before them. Though only two years old, she could already type all twenty-six English letters on a keyboard, astonishing everyone present. I had always believed she was painfully introverted. Yet that evening she somehow became the wildest child in the room. Several colleagues even laughed and said: “Your ‘son’ is so naughty!” But the greatest surprise of all came when she overcame her fear and voluntarily walked onto the stage to present flowers. That night brought me so much joy and astonishment. Perhaps my daughter still possessed hidden talents waiting quietly to be discovered.

Birthdays were her favorite thing in the world. Cake. Candles. Celebration. When she turned three, I happened to be away on a business trip and was originally unable to come home. But for her sake, I moved mountains to return, finally arriving home at ten-thirty that night. She had already been sleepy. Yet the moment she saw me, her eyes lit up with pure excitement and joy — a look I can still see vividly today. What moved me most was what my husband told me afterward. Apparently she had spent the entire evening crouched beside the cake, sniffing it longingly with her little nose. Yet when her father, unable to bear her suffering, suggested cutting her a tiny piece first, she refused absolutely. “No. Nobody touches it. We must wait for Mommy to come home and see it first. And only Mommy can cut it.” So she waited. And waited. And waited for me until ten-thirty at night. Even now, remembering it still brings tears to my eyes.

Her welcome-home rituals were equally unforgettable. According to my husband, whenever the doorbell rang and she opened the door, he would first see a face glowing with excitement — until, less than a second later, her expression collapsed in disappointment. “Oh. It’s you.” Poor man. No matter how hard he tried — emotionally, materially, strategically — he could never quite win her heart. My treatment, however, was entirely different. Whenever I came home, she would fly toward me like a little butterfly, wrap herself around me, press her cheek against me, and call softly: “Mommy…” Then she would hurry to place slippers at my feet, remove my handbag from my shoulder, and struggle heroically to drag my heavy computer bag into the house. Meanwhile she would shout commands at her father: “Daddy! Pour Mommy some water! Quickly!” And her father could only stand there shaking his head with helpless amusement.

She always remembered my birthday perfectly. Whenever my birthday approached, she became the busiest and happiest person in the family, managing everything like the unquestioned head of the household. Twice I was overseas and unable to return home for my birthday. After missing the first one, she took matters into her own hands the second time and decided I should celebrate according to the lunar calendar instead. One year, she even insisted the family search several stores for an ice cream cake I had once casually mentioned liking. She remembered it forever simply because I had once said I liked it. And yet she herself loved ice cream cakes too. Why had I never thought to buy one for her birthdays?

Last Christmas, I bought her a Christmas tree. This Christmas, when the lights were glowing softly, she took my hand and led me into her room to show me something. On the table lay a crude little handwritten note:

“XXX, (she called me by my full name)
I am sheng dan lao ren (Santa Claus).
You wei wo fu chu le hen duo (you have sacrificed so much for me).
I song gei ni liang kuai tang (give you two candies).” Beside the note lay two White Rabbit candies.

The note was written exactly like that — a mixture of Chinese characters and pinyin. Later she realized herself that she should not have written “for me,” since she was pretending to be Santa Claus and should have written “for your daughter.” But it was precisely that instinctive, heartfelt “for me” that moved me for a very, very long time. My daughter. In that moment, all the years I had spent raising you suddenly felt insignificant. And the love you gave me — pure, wholehearted, innocent love — became something I knew I would never forget for the rest of my life.

The truth is, I often felt unworthy of it. There were times I scolded her for calling me too often while I was busy working. Times I promised to take her to museums, theaters, or parks, only to cancel because of work. Once I even arrived five minutes late picking her up, leaving her standing helplessly outside waiting for me. And there were countless moments when I should have spent more time simply playing with her. Yet she was always the one comforting me. After arguments between my husband and me, she would wipe away my tears and whisper gently: “Don’t take it to heart.” Sometimes she gave me handmade cards. At the top she wrote “FROM” beside her own name. At the bottom, “TO” beside mine. And between them, always a heart. Sometimes, when I was exhausted in the car, she would offer me her tiny shoulder to lean on. And when I protested that she was too small, she would sit up as straight as possible, deliberately trying to make herself taller for me.

Writing this now, my eyes fill with tears again. People often ask me what expectations I have for my daughter — what kind of person I hope she becomes. To my angel, I want to say this: I ask nothing of you. I only hope you grow up healthy and happy. Truly. Health and happiness are enough. Nothing else matters.

I remember something Yao Ming once said: “McGrady is the flower. Forget being a leaf — I’d even be willing to be the soil beneath him.” And what I want my precious daughter to know is this: As writer Chi Li once wrote, “If a rose requires fertile soil in order to bloom, then I would willingly become immortal beneath its roots.”

Written in 2006.
