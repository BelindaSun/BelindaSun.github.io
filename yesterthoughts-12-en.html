You Are My Angel Forever (Part 1)

Preface

My daughter had been unusually well-behaved these past few days, and I couldn’t quite figure out why. Finally, she explained:
“Because Mommy promised to buy me a Christmas present if I behave!”
“So after Christmas, you won’t behave anymore?”
“I will!”
I had barely opened my mouth to praise her when she added thoughtfully:
“Because then it’ll be New Year’s.”

Moments like these were endless, yet I had never written them down. Mostly because words had never been my strength, much less writing. And yet, over the past few days, I felt an inexplicable urge to leave her something in words.
So these pages came into being.
I wrote whatever came to mind, wherever my thoughts wandered. At times the memories may tumble out chaotically; at times the stories may refuse to connect neatly. But I think no matter how imperfect these words are, my daughter will love them anyway.
Don’t you think so?

I. An Angel Arrives

At first, I had hoped for a son.
Not for any grand reason. I simply imagined that if our family had two men in the house, I would surely be spoiled and protected forever. Whenever danger appeared, the two of them would leap forward in unison and declare:
“I’ll protect you!”
Even imagining it felt intoxicatingly wonderful.

But later I realized God sees through everything. He never allowed my little conspiracy to succeed.
When I was eight months pregnant, an ultrasound revealed the truth. That day, I nervously asked the doctor the baby’s gender. I had assumed doctors at Peking Union Medical College Hospital would refuse to tell me, but the doctor answered cheerfully and directly:
“A girl!”

I was a little disappointed — the first genuine disappointment I’d felt throughout the entire pregnancy.
I called my husband immediately. Over the phone, he comforted me gently:
“It’s fine. Girls are wonderful too.”
Well, what choice did I have? Besides, according to Old Xu’s so-called “expert theories,” our family was destined to have a daughter anyway. Honestly, if he had conducted his research a few years earlier, I could have spared myself all those unrealistic fantasies and avoided this crushing disappointment now.

Still, once the mystery was solved, my heart settled down. Since there was no longer suspense about the baby’s gender, I created a new suspense for myself:
What would she look like?
But the more I imagined, the sadder I became. Whoever she resembled, how could she possibly turn out beautiful?
My husband, however, was full of confidence.
“If she looks like her father, don’t worry — our daughter will definitely be pretty.”
I looked up at him.
And felt even more distressed.

My daughter’s birth was anything but smooth.
There was still a week before my due date, yet I was unexpectedly admitted to the hospital early. Family visits weren’t allowed, so from then on my husband and I communicated through handwritten notes.
Being naturally gifted with words, he even managed to make little scraps of paper sound literary and elegant. Reading them should have been delightful. But at the time, I was drowning in fear and loneliness, so completely consumed by them that I remember nothing he actually wrote.

What irritated me most was this:
On the very day our daughter was born, he wasn’t even at the hospital.
To be fair, it wasn’t entirely his fault. I hadn’t yet reached my due date, and the doctor had suddenly decided to induce labor. The nurse simply forgot to notify him.
So while I lay on the delivery bed in agony, I could vividly picture him somewhere in front of his computer, fully absorbed — either reverse-engineering something or studying the Windows kernel yet again.
The more I imagined it, the angrier I became.
And the angrier I became, the more it hurt.

Our daughter was born at 10:15 p.m. on October 19, 1998.
Fifty-four centimeters long. Three thousand nine hundred and fifty grams.
I was utterly exhausted, drifting in and out of consciousness. One thought looped endlessly in my head:
Thank God. It’s finally over. Boy or girl, who cares? I’m free at last.
Honestly, journalists should have interviewed me right then. I would probably have declared tearfully and sincerely:
“The government is absolutely right — one child per family is more than enough. Beat me to death and I still won’t have a second one.”

My daughter cried only a few times before opening her eyes.
I heard voices around me exclaiming:
“Look! Look at her eyes — they’re huge! Her pupils are already moving!”
“She’s beautiful. She looks exactly like an angel.”
My husband rushed to the hospital as soon as he heard the news, utterly enchanted by his beautiful little angel.

As for me, I didn’t even get a proper look at her that first night.
I never slept.
I was exhausted and in pain. Babies cried one after another throughout the ward without pause. Nurses rushed in and out. Lights flickered on and off all night long.
And yet my daughter lay there quietly the entire night.
No crying. No fussing. No disturbance at all.
It was as though from the very first day of her life, she already understood how to love her mother — a tiny angel who knew instinctively how to spare me even a little suffering.

II. At Last, I Saw My Angel’s Face

One of my male classmates once described seeing his newborn son for the first time:
“I waited outside anxiously forever. Finally the nurse carried him out and handed him toward me. I took one look and froze. Was that really my son? He looked exactly like a tiny old man — wrinkled all over. I was so shocked I didn’t even take him.”

Honestly, newborn babies can look like almost anything, so I had prepared myself mentally.
The next day, my husband carefully carried our daughter over to my bed as though he were transporting a live bomb. And for the first time, I truly saw her.
I had imagined every possible version of her.
Except this one.
Never had I imagined she could be so beautiful.
Calling her an angel was not an exaggeration at all.
Fresh from her bath, her hair was jet black with the slightest natural curl. Her eyes were enormous, deeply set, with gleaming dark pupils. Her tiny face glowed rosy pink, fair and luminous in a way that seemed almost unreal. Even her lips were soft pink.
I was overjoyed.
Wonderful, I thought. In the future I won’t even need to buy her lipstick.

Then I hurriedly inspected her from head to toe, carefully checking every finger and every toe. The little “doll” was perfectly intact.
My heart overflowed with happiness and astonishment.
The pain of the previous day faded instantly into the background. My old desire for a son disappeared completely.
Because when it is your own child, none of those things truly matter — not gender, not appearance. Health and happiness are enough.
And ours just happened to be an unbearably adorable little doll besides.

Even now, my husband still marvels at how angelic she looked at birth.
My mother, meanwhile, never stops saying that our daughter must be the blessing accumulated through generations of good fortune.
Later my mother confessed that while I was pregnant, she had secretly worried to my older sister:
“With those two as parents, imagine how unfortunate-looking the baby might turn out…”
Honestly! My mother was ridiculous.
Fine, I admit my husband isn’t exactly handsome. But me? I may not be beautiful, but surely I’m not that bad-looking… right? (Though admittedly, not with overwhelming confidence.)
She even claimed my sister had been so ugly as a child that she felt embarrassed taking her outside — despite the fact my sister later became the school beauty.
Now, finally, I could confront her triumphantly:
“Well? Weren’t you worrying for nothing? Isn’t my daughter prettier than yours?”
On this issue, I am forever the winner.
Because either answer benefits me.

Of course, our daughter’s beauty didn’t come entirely from her father. I’m self-aware enough not to claim all the credit for myself.
She simply inherited the best features from everyone and improved upon them.
Her fair skin came from her grandmother. Her slightly deep-set eyes from her grandfather. Her large eyes and double eyelids from her father.
As for me, after much careful study, I finally discovered that the shape of her eyes — and especially her expressions — resembled mine. That comforted me greatly.
Her only flaw was a slightly prominent forehead — what we call a “big brow bone.” To this day, no one has officially admitted where she inherited it from.
Though honestly, the answer is obvious.
That particular person simply refuses to confess.

Yet less than a day into motherhood, I discovered something important:
Even angels are not always beautiful, serene, and understanding.
By the second night, trouble had already come knocking at my door.

III. The Troubles That Follow Happiness

Before marriage, whenever I saw pregnant women, I instinctively pitied them.
Then I became one myself and suddenly discovered pregnancy was actually a strangely fulfilling happiness.
You become inexplicably cheerful. Sometimes you even burst into laughter for no reason at all. Housework becomes optional. Work expectations soften. You are suddenly allowed to indulge yourself in ways the world normally forbids.
Strangers treat you kindly. People yield to you everywhere.
It feels as though the entire world has become gentler toward you.
I had spent years longing for that feeling without realizing it might someday truly arrive.

One of my girlfriends once enjoyed — and tragically lost — this privilege briefly.
Thinking she was pregnant, her husband happily took over all the housework and showered her with affection. Later they discovered it had merely been a false alarm.
Her husband exploded:
“So that’s what this was? You tricked me into feeding you, serving you, and doing all the chores? Enough already — go cook dinner!”

Honestly, before my daughter was born, life felt extraordinarily happy and complete. Looking back now, those few months may well have been the happiest period of my life so far.
Perhaps hormones deserve all the credit.
Pregnancy was kind to me too. I never suffered morning sickness. I simply lost my appetite at first and occasionally woke in the middle of the night starving.
Sometimes I would panic from hunger and shake my husband awake:
“Darling… I’m sorry… I think the baby might have starved to death…”

But enough rambling.
Let me continue with the endless “troubles” that followed happiness.
Most families’ troubles begin once the child arrives.
Mine began on the second night.
My little angel transformed completely from the calm, quiet newborn of the first day. Suddenly she cried nonstop.
I had no idea what was wrong.
The moment I picked her up, she stopped crying.
The moment I put her down, she wailed again.
Pick up: silence.
Put down: crying.
Who installed this automatic switch?
In the end, weak and exhausted beyond words, I sat there holding her the entire night.
What puzzled me most was this: how could other mothers lie there peacefully sleeping while their own babies cried endlessly nearby?
I simply couldn’t do it.

Later I realized this was only the first step of a very, very long march.
Unexpected exhaustion, anxiety, hardship, and chaos followed one after another.
At the hospital, nurses helped.
At home, reality struck.
We had learned a few techniques from the nurses and immediately began practicing them in total panic and confusion.
Changing diapers was especially ridiculous.
Trusting the nurses far too much, we became convinced disposable diapers were bad for babies, so we used old-fashioned cloth inserts instead. My poor husband searched half of Beijing to buy them.
But they were tiny and constantly needed changing.
I would barely finish replacing one before the baby peed again — often before I even completed step two of the nurse’s elaborate four-step “baby wrapping procedure.”
Everything had to start over from the beginning.
Work had never defeated me before.
Yet this tiny little creature left me utterly helpless.

Thankfully, our family helped tremendously. I barely needed to do anything except feed her, give her water, and tuck her in.
My husband deserves special mention.
Over six feet tall, he somehow couldn’t figure out how to hold an eight-pound baby. She was too soft, too fragile.
Eventually he invented his own method:
First place the baby on a large pillow.
Then carry both the pillow and the baby together.

A few months later, our little angel developed another fascinating habit.
Every morning at exactly five o’clock, she woke up full of energy.
Seeing that I had only just fallen asleep, she decided to entertain herself by “talking” or singing loudly in her own mysterious baby language.
Very loudly.
So loudly that I genuinely worried neighbors might come knock on the door.
She would lie there chatting away to herself, asking and answering her own questions, thoroughly enjoying herself.
Even now I remember it vividly.
Painful as it was to be awakened just as sleep finally arrived, it was also unbelievably funny.

Later things escalated further.
When she was around one year old, she developed a terrifying summer habit: waking at two or three in the morning demanding,
“Go outside! Go outside!”
Meaning she refused to stay indoors and wanted a stroll.
Imagine that — needing walks earlier than a puppy.
So there we were in the middle of the night:
One person pushing the stroller.
One person fanning her.
One person carrying water.
One person trailing behind.
Honestly, even mafia bosses don’t travel with that level of entourage.
Meanwhile the little female boss reclined comfortably in the stroller, occasionally opening one eye to supervise us.
I would silently beg:
Please go back to sleep. I still have to work tomorrow.
You slept all day long and now you’re walking us at night. Is this remotely reasonable?

Later, when she was a bit older, we took her horse riding at Mulan Paddock. She was too scared to ride alone, so a colleague suggested the two of us share a horse. At first she was tense, then slowly relaxed, and then became so addicted that she didn’t even want me with her anymore — to be fair, sharing a horse wasn’t comfortable for either of us.
So there I was, walking miserably behind the horse through the scorching sun, across endless grasslands and sand dunes, bitten by mosquitoes, forced to match the horse’s pace and her endless demands.
We walked for several kilometers.
Honestly, whenever Mulan Paddock is mentioned, I still flinch.

Those things I could eventually laugh about. But the lost sleep became a lifelong problem.
Just imagine: I woke up more than ten times every single night — to feed her, give her water, tuck her in. Every time she turned over, kicked her legs, raised an arm, stretched lazily — I would wake up. And sometimes, once I woke up, I couldn’t fall back asleep.
Over the years, my English listening didn’t improve much, but another kind of listening advanced to an almost supernatural level. Even the faintest sound — a single dropped needle — I could hear clearly.
I tried to stop being so hyper‑aware of her. But motherhood isn’t something you can turn off so easily.

Beyond sleep, social life nearly disappeared.
I had more than fifty university and graduate school classmates living in Beijing. Yet in the first four or five years after my daughter was born, I attended practically no gatherings at all. Whether the outside world was wonderful or unbearable, I simply didn’t know anymore. Even now I still don’t fully understand how one tiny creature could consume so much time and freedom.

And then there was the loss of life and space.
I used to love so many things: movies, books, television, plays, card games, parties, chatting, traveling. But for years, I had almost no chance to enjoy any of them. Two‑person worlds and freedom became distant luxuries. There was no one else but her. Even my card‑game addiction — I used to be the “always needing three more” player — completely disappeared.
Instead, I developed a whole new set of “hobbies” just to keep up with her. I, who had never enjoyed exercise, found myself running, jumping rope, playing ball, making up games, going to parks, watching performances… and it looked like this might go on forever.

Yet somehow, despite all the exhaustion, all the chaos, all the sacrifices, she continued growing day by day, bringing endless surprises and moments of tenderness.
And I, like the Little Prince watching over his rose, found myself quietly waiting for her to bloom.
That anticipation itself was beautiful.
And when one day the flower finally blossoms, who still remembers all the hardship and sorrow that came before?
