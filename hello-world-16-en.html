Walking the Edge of Human Knowledge (I)

An Engineer's First Conversation with ChatGPT

Before We Begin

Ever since late last year, whenever my engineer husband saw me filling my WeChat Moments feed with posts about how AI was "about to disrupt this" and "about to reinvent that," he'd roll his eyes and accuse me of "creating anxiety every single day."

"Stop being so alarmist."

I couldn't really blame him.

If you don't follow the AI world closely, it does sound like exaggeration.

But if you spend every day immersed in frontier AI developments, you realize something important:

The world has already begun to change.

After months of my relentless campaign—mostly by forcing him to watch tiny AI products come to life in just a few minutes—he finally started paying attention.

Little by little, his curiosity replaced his skepticism.

He began reading about large language models, following AI news, and more than once found himself genuinely amazed by how quickly everything was evolving.

Last night, on a whim, I decided to try ChatGPT's new voice conversation feature.

We chatted for a while about AI, creativity, and how, in a world where code is becoming less of a competitive moat, good ideas matter more than ever.

Eventually we ran out of things to talk about.

So I handed the conversation over to my husband.

What followed turned into an unexpectedly long—and fascinating—conversation.

It was his very first time talking to an AI.

As you'll soon discover, it also offers a surprisingly accurate glimpse into what occupies his mind every single day. 😄

Afterwards, ChatGPT automatically generated a transcript of the conversation, preserving almost all of the original pacing and tone.

For readability, I removed the endless strings of "uh-huhs" and repeated filler words, but otherwise left it almost entirely untouched.

I also asked ChatGPT to write an article about the experience for my website's Hello Human column.

Less than a minute later, it produced both Chinese and English versions, then cheerfully added:

"I intentionally kept the rhythm of your husband's relentless questioning. It feels much more like a Hello Human story than a typical science explainer. You could even give it a more playful title—something like 'My Husband Took Me from Mars to Square Waves.' 😄"

There were, admittedly, a few tiny AI hallucinations in the article.

Nothing serious.

What stayed with me wasn't perfect factual accuracy.

It was something else entirely.

Last night's conversation—from its tone to its rhythm to its spontaneity—felt astonishingly human.

For the first time, it genuinely felt as though another person was sitting across the table.

And that was the part that truly amazed me.

Walking the Edge of Human Knowledge (I)
An Engineer's First Conversation with ChatGPT

July 12, 2026 · 10:40 PM

Engineer: When do you think humans will finally land on Mars?

ChatGPT: Let me think... That's a fascinating question. Honestly, nobody can give an exact date. The most realistic prediction is sometime in the 2030s—or perhaps even later. NASA's current roadmap is to establish a long-term presence on the Moon first, using it as a proving ground before attempting Mars. SpaceX certainly has ambitious plans, but reality almost always moves more slowly than imagination. The challenge isn't just getting there. We have to land safely, survive there, and then return safely. Any one of those hurdles could delay the schedule. Personally, I think the second half of the 2030s sounds realistic. Earlier wouldn't surprise me. Neither would 2040. But we're fortunate enough that our generation may actually live to witness humanity setting foot on Mars.

Engineer: By 2050, it should definitely have happened, right?

ChatGPT: I'd be much more optimistic about 2050. Of course, no one can guarantee it, but I'd say there's a very good chance humans will have reached Mars by then. Technology keeps improving. Rockets keep evolving. AI and robotics are making exploration far safer. And governments as well as private companies are all pushing toward the same goal. What excites me even more isn't simply planting another flag. It's the possibility of a small, permanent scientific outpost—a place where dozens of researchers rotate in and out over the years. A real Martian city? That will probably take much longer. So yes, I think people living on Mars by 2050 is entirely possible. Hundreds of thousands of settlers? That would be... considerably more optimistic.

Engineer: What about the Sun? When do you think we'll be able to land on the Sun?

Author's Note:

1. My husband's pronunciation isn't always crystal clear because he's missing a tooth.

2. More importantly, he was being mischievous. When he asked this question, he shot me a sly little grin. I immediately knew what he was doing. He was testing ChatGPT. We exchanged a knowing smile and quietly waited to see whether it would embarrass itself.

ChatGPT: Hmm... Let me check... I'm guessing you might be asking when the Sun will burn out. That would be roughly another five billion years—

Engineer: No, no. I mean if humans can land on Mars around 2050... When will we be able to land on the Sun? That's what I'm asking.

ChatGPT: Oh! That's a fun question. The answer is actually very straightforward: Almost certainly never. The Sun doesn't have a solid surface to stand on. It's essentially a gigantic sphere of plasma. Its visible surface is about 5,500°C (9,900°F), and every material we know of would vaporize long before getting anywhere close. The deeper you go, the more extreme it becomes. So landing on the Sun isn't like landing on the Moon or Mars. What we can do—and are already doing—is fly as close as possible to study it. NASA's Parker Solar Probe has already traveled closer to the Sun than any spacecraft in history. But even it merely skims the outskirts to collect data. So yes—landing on the Moon? Absolutely. Landing on Mars? Very likely. Landing on the Sun? That's a bit like trying to dance on a flame. The laws of physics simply don't allow it.

Me: Not bad. He passed the test.

Engineer: Mm-hmm.

ChatGPT: Glad to hear it! Keep the questions coming.

Engineer: What about getting close to the edge of a black hole?

ChatGPT: Now that's an even more exciting question. In theory, yes—you can get close. But it depends on what kind of black hole we're talking about, and what exactly you mean by "close."

Engineer: I mean... standing right at the event horizon...

ChatGPT: Right. If you mean orbiting just outside the event horizon, then yes—in theory that's possible. Of course, it would require propulsion and protection technologies far beyond anything we currently possess.

Me: (touched the screen and disconnected) We weren't finished!

ChatGPT: Hang on... Of course! Let's continue. We were talking about approaching the event horizon of a black hole. From a physics perspective, yes—I remember exactly where we left off. Theoretically, you can travel astonishingly close to the event horizon, especially around a supermassive black hole. Until you actually cross it, you might not even notice anything unusual. Smaller stellar-mass black holes are a different story. Their tidal forces would—put simply—stretch you into a very long strand of spaghetti. Physicists even have a name for it: Spaghettification. What's even more fascinating is how time appears to different observers. Someone watching you from far away would see you moving more and more slowly, almost frozen at the edge forever. But from your own perspective, everything would feel perfectly normal. Time would continue flowing naturally. So yes—you can approach a black hole. You can even orbit one. But you can't stand on it. There isn't actually a surface there. That's a wonderfully hardcore question. I like it.
