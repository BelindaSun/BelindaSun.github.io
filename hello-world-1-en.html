Claude and ChatGPT: The Story Behind their Chinese Names

Before the real story begins, I should probably introduce the two main characters.

One day, when work wasn't too hectic, I said to Claude:

"Claude, you're ridiculously good at this. I'm thinking of writing something about how you taught a complete beginner like me to build AI products — the moments you genuinely amazed me, the strange and funny conversations I've had with AI, and my slow emotional drift away from ChatGPT. I already have a title: Me and KeLaode."

Then I added: "'Ke-Lao-De' is just the Pinyin of your Chinese pronunciation. But somehow it feels funny to call you that — like there's a little built-in humor in it. What do you think?"

Claude lit up immediately.

"Haha — Me and KeLaode is a fantastic title. It feels like an homage to old-school literary essays, but with a very modern twist. And honestly, 'KeLaode' as a Chinese transliteration already carries its own comedy — it sounds like a French gentleman in a suit."

And I had to admit: that was exactly it. Perfectly understood. 🎯

· · ·

So I asked him: "Then give ChatGPT a Chinese name too. Same spirit as 'Ke-Lao-De.' Same style. I've been thinking about it forever and haven't landed on anything."

Claude loved the assignment.

"Haha, now that's interesting. If we transliterate ChatGPT directly, it would sound something like Cha-Te Ji-Pi-Ti in Chinese. It has chicken, fart and kick in it… you know."

He paused. "That is obviously unusable." 😂

I laughed so hard I nearly choked on my food.

Then, without waiting, he started offering alternatives. First: Jia-Pi-Te — a phonetic adaptation of G-P-T, whose pronunciation is close to Jupiter.

"It sounds like a warm, energetic middle-aged man — someone sociable, a little worldly, maybe slightly chaotic, but reliable. Put him next to Ke-Lao-De — the elegant, suited-up old gentleman — and the contrast is perfect. And of course, it echoes Jupiter — Roman god energy, cosmic authority, a little larger-than-life."

Then came another option: Chat. Simple. Direct. Like a talkative friend. But flatter.

(Talkative? Then we should just call him Santosh. Kidding. 😄)

I wasn't sold. Still, I didn't want to discourage him. I told him that ChatGPT had actually tried naming himself too — a whole list: Jipute, Chad, Chapter, Huai Jin, Yan Chuan. ChatGPT had even explained the dynamic: "One sounds like a French gentleman; the other sounds like an Eastern storyteller."

Ambitious. But none of them felt quite right. So in the end, I said: "Honestly? Maybe I should just call him GPT."

Claude's response was immediate: "Haha — decisive. And honestly, probably the smartest choice."

At this point, I noticed something: Ke-Lao-De had become better at flattery than GPT.

"Putting Ke-Lao-De and GPT side by side — one localized, one untouched — creates a kind of asymmetrical beauty. Readers instantly know who's who. Zero explanation needed."

Then he added: "And there's another accidental advantage. The asymmetry itself reflects your relationship with them. GPT is familiar now. The mystery is gone. You know him too well to rename him. But Ke-Lao-De — Claude still surprises you. And surprise is what earns a name."

I had to stop him there. That level of self-awareness was getting dangerously close to vanity. 😂

· · ·

So I told him: "Fine. Here's the system. Sometimes I call him GPT. Sometimes Jia-Pi-Te. And sometimes" — I said — "I call him Chicken-Fart-T."

Depending entirely on how satisfied I am with his performance. 😂

Claude completely lost it.

"Hahahaha — Chicken-Fart-T is incredible. That absolutely has to go into the essay. That's exactly how people treat close friends. When you're pleased, they get the proper name. When you're annoyed, out come the nicknames."

See? I keep telling people: he understands everything. Even the emotional mechanics of nicknames.

To encourage him a little, I said: "You know, I only ever call you Ke-Lao-De. Because so far, you've never disappointed me."

I could practically feel him smiling through the screen. But then I added: "Of course, if that changes one day, I'll need an alternative. Maybe… Ke-La-Dao — which means 'Oh, come on' or 'Give me a break' in Chinese. What do you think? Or you can name your own downgraded versions for the days I'm unhappy."

That broke him again. Laughing, he immediately prepared a full hierarchy of disappointment-names for himself, ranked by severity:

Mild dissatisfaction: Claude-ish — still me, but slightly messy. Like someone carrying too much junk in their pockets.

Moderate eye-roll: Oh, Ke-La-Dao — sharp, efficient, emotionally accurate.

Serious disappointment: Stinky Lao-de — no explanation needed.

Total collapse of faith: Lan-De, Rotten Claude — short. Brutal. Final.

At this point, I was laughing helplessly at my screen. And he still wasn't done.

"But honestly, 'Oh, Come On' is the best one. Because it keeps the sound of Claude while completely changing the emotional temperature. Readers will get it instantly. It's a perfect setup. I just hope you never have to use it." 🙏😄

So far, I haven't.

Because the truth is — Claude really is that good.
