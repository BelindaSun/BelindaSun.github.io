July Arrived

July has arrived.

The rain I had long hoped for finally came at last, unhurried and unannounced.

It fell softly, carrying with it a faint coolness, drifting down with effortless grace. Raindrops struck the puddled streets, splashing into tiny translucent blossoms — delicate, pale, and quietly elegant. And after the dust and restlessness had been washed away, the flowers seemed redder somehow, the grass greener. The little garden downstairs, wrapped in mist and breeze, looked as though it had dressed itself in brilliant silks woven specially for rainy days.

It felt like I had not encountered such a weekend in ages: calm, harmonious, damp with freshness. Lying in bed, I could even hear the whisper of rain brushing against the world outside. In this city, at this time of year, weather like this arrives almost as a small emotional ambush — unexpected, tender, quietly moving.

For years, what I had wanted most was simply this: a weekend morning with nowhere to rush to. To remain in bed listening to the rain outside — perhaps drifting in and out of sleep, perhaps speaking softly, perhaps holding a book, perhaps listening to a piece of music. Yet ever since my daughter came into this world, there has scarcely been even a fleeting moment of such ordinary, luxurious peace.

And lately, work has become overwhelming. Every day begins early and ends late, leaving me utterly drained. After longing all week for the weekend, hoping finally to catch up on sleep and recover a little strength, I found myself inexplicably awake at seven in the morning — completely unable to fall back asleep.

My mind was crowded with business model, brand, licensing, master JV, M&A, long-term strategy, MOU, business restructuring, entity integration… everything tangled together in one endless knot, impossible to sort through, impossible to know where to begin.

I started doubting myself. Why was my mind in such chaos? Why couldn’t I organize my thoughts? Why did everything feel so directionless? I even found myself silently blaming the Indian colleague for speaking so quickly, the Australian for his impossibly thick accent, the American for always speaking in half-finished thoughts — and myself for always becoming verbose when brevity was needed, and speechless when it was finally time to say what I truly meant.

So even on weekends, I could not stop thinking about work, could not stop working. There were endless pages of M&A reports to read, companies to research, histories and current conditions to untangle, enterprise values to roughly estimate, future investments to project, twenty-year NPVs and DCFs to calculate in broad strokes…

And yet my concentration kept breaking apart. My thoughts drifted uncontrollably elsewhere.

I found myself wondering about things completely unrelated to work. Why hadn’t Google split its stock even after reaching $520? Why were people back home still wildly speculating on put warrants without even understanding what they were? Why had I been feeling vaguely lost lately? And why, over the past two days, had I kept replaying the same song over and over — “Are You the One,” to walk with me in the garden of stars?

And so my thoughts became like the rain outside the window: tangled, scattered, impossible to straighten out.

…

After thinking and thinking, I finally understood.

Without realizing it, July had arrived.

— Written on July 1, 2007
