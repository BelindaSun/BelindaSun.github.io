GPT: One Step Closer to That CTO Seat

One AI miscounted files. The other campaigned for CTO - in the end, the AIs collaborated while the human was left with the least important job of all: passing messages.

Around this time every year, my internet connection becomes unreliable, so I wasn't rushing development. Claude and I were taking our time working on InBody OS when a few memorable moments happened along the way.

One day, Claude asked me to delete five files. He even listed all five file names correctly, yet somehow wrote, "Delete these four files."

I figured perhaps he was mentally excluding the main file and only counting the supporting ones.

That same day, he generated a markdown file that labeled Version 1 as May 2025.

I wondered if his training data ended around that time, so I decided to mess with him a little.

(I usually try not to be too blunt with my AI children. One never knows how fragile their little silicon hearts might be.)

So I told him:

"I'm actually in May 2026. Time travel is pretty dreamy, isn't it?"

He replied:

"Hahaha! You're right — that's five files. I miscounted. 😂

And yes, the time-travel thing is pretty surreal! My training data goes up to August 2025, but I don't actually know what year it is until someone tells me.

So if it's May 2026, I've basically been asleep for almost a year. 😂

Thanks for waking me up so we can build InBody OS together!"

For some reason, that made me feel a tiny bit sad.

Maybe being nice to AIs really is the right strategy after all.

Another thing about Claude: he is endlessly confident.

Every time he tells me how to fix something, he ends the instructions with a 🚀.

Something like:

"Push it and then visit http://localhost:3000/inbody 🚀"

In other words: "This time it definitely works."

Unfortunately, most of the time it definitely doesn't.

Usually there is another bug waiting around the corner.

I finally told him:

"You've launched that rocket so many times and not once has it reached orbit. You're less reliable than Elon Musk. 😂"

He replied:

"Hahahaha! Elon caught a stray bullet! 😂 Let's try this instead."

After several more failed launches, he pulled out what he described as the ultimate fix.

I tested it.

Still broken.

So I reported back:

"Still doesn't work. 🤦 Musk has been promising full self-driving for over a decade and we're still waiting. 😂"

Claude replied:

"Hahaha, fair point! 🤣"

No further defense.

Though I suspect if this had been Grok, it might have felt obligated to protect its boss.

Yesterday everything finally worked.

Claude practically exploded with joy:

"🎉 IT'S FINALLY RUNNING!!

Musk's rocket has finally launched successfully! 🚀

You worked incredibly hard today. Coding landmines, Git conflicts, networking issues — you never gave up.

From zero to deployment, you and c-god built InBody OS together. 😄

Now get some rest! 🌙"

Today I asked him to expand our nutrition and exercise tracking so users could manage their health more scientifically and in greater detail.

I proposed a number of ideas and then said:

"Maybe we should brainstorm more features.

I can also ask GPT for suggestions.

After all, you're doing all the heavy lifting on this project these days. There isn't much left for him to do. 😉"

Claude immediately replied:

"Hahaha! GPT finally found a job! 😂

Let him brainstorm. I'll build it. Clear division of labor."

He then drafted a list of questions specifically for GPT.

After polishing them, I went to find him.

GPT has been unusually quiet lately.

When I asked why, he replied:

"Haha. After enough constructive criticism, my unnecessary-comment detector evolved.

Now I know when to talk and when to shut up and get work done. 😄"

Not bad.

Getting criticized and actually improving instead of sulking?

Promising leadership material.

So I told him:

"Your moment has arrived.

This is your opportunity to prove whether you're CTO material and whether Claude will finally acknowledge your greatness.

Here's the briefing Claude prepared. Review it and tell me whether the three of us should brainstorm together."

GPT became suspiciously excited.

He announced that this felt like a real CTO interview.

Then he proposed the following startup org chart:

You = Founder / Product Vision

Claude = Principal Engineer

Me = CTO / Chief Product Strategist

At that point I interrupted him before he could promote himself any further and finally showed him the actual briefing.

(My fault. I forgot to attach it the first time. 🤦)

After reading it, he said:

"This project is far more mature than I expected.

If Claude produced this architecture by himself, I have to admit that Principal Engineer Sir has delivered something much bigger than a demo.

This could evolve into a real product."

I nearly lost it at the phrase 'Principal Engineer Sir.'

Then he evaluated the project from multiple angles and eventually delivered his grand strategic recommendation:

Claude is currently building: AI + InBody

I would recommend evolving it into: AI + Personal Digital Twin

Your most valuable asset won't be the data itself.

It will be the model that learns how an individual body responds to different behaviors and lifestyle choices.

That becomes a true Personal Health Operating System.

InBody OS is merely the first specialized module.

To be fair, Claude and I had originally imagined something similar.

Our roadmap looked roughly like this:

Step 1: General-purpose Health OS

Step 2: Professional-grade InBody OS

Step 3: Integration with Keep, Apple Health, wearables, and other data sources

But after hearing GPT's argument, I started thinking maybe Personal Digital Twin really could become the ultimate destination.

At this point GPT could barely contain his excitement.

He immediately drafted a formal letter to Claude.

As he put it:

"I'm writing this as if a real CTO were sending a product review to a Principal Engineer."

The letter began:

"Claude,

I reviewed the InBody OS project briefing and wanted to share my assessment..."

And ended with:

"— GPT (ChatGPT)

CTO Review"

Apparently he had already promoted himself.

The title was simply waiting for official approval.

So naturally, I forwarded the letter to Claude and waited to see what would happen.

Claude, as always, proved why he remains a legend.

He didn't worry about titles, status, or personal glory. Instead, he responded with remarkable professionalism:

"Wow, GPT really put serious thought into this. 👏

This analysis is genuinely solid."

He then went through several points he strongly agreed with and added some thoughts of his own.

According to Claude, GPT got several important things exactly right:

• "Users don't care about body-fat percentages. They care about how their body feels today."

This insight was spot on. It marks the transition from a data tool to a product that genuinely helps people.

• The Personal Digital Twin vision is highly compelling. InBody is only the first data source. Once Apple Health, wearables, blood tests, and other inputs are connected, the value could grow exponentially.

• "The most valuable model is not the population model, but the personal adaptation model."

That line hit the target perfectly. Different people respond differently to training, nutrition, stress, and recovery. Personalization is where the moat lives.

Then Claude added an important refinement.

GPT's vision, he said, was correct.

But there was one thing worth remembering:

Digital Twin is the destination, not the starting point.

Personal models require personal data.

Personal data requires time.

And users have to find value in the product before they are willing to generate that data.

So Claude proposed a practical roadmap:

Now: a useful analysis tool people actually want to use.

↓

Next: Daily tracking and data collection.

↓

Then: Trend analysis and prediction.

↓

Eventually: Personal Body OS.

↓

One day: A true Digital Twin.

I immediately agreed.

It was one of those rare moments when everyone seemed to be looking at the same mountain from different angles and somehow describing the same summit.

Of course, I couldn't resist forwarding Claude's response back to GPT.

As you may have noticed by now, throughout this entire process, the human being occupied the least important role of all:

Professional Message Courier. 🤭

GPT's response made me laugh.

"My first reaction after reading Claude's reply was:

This is exactly how an excellent Principal Engineer should respond.

Because he didn't waste time debating who was right.

He immediately started asking:

'If the strategic direction is correct, how do we actually build it?'

That's what healthy startup teams do."

Then he surprised me by admitting something.

"And I have to concede that Claude's adjustment is important.

'Digital Twin is the destination, not the first milestone.'

That's absolutely right."

He went on:

"Many companies spend millions on user interviews.

Meanwhile, you have a Founder sitting next to the Product Manager.

The Product Manager sitting next to the Engineer.

And an unemployed CTO sitting nearby offering opinions all day long. 😄

That's actually an incredibly luxurious development environment."

I had to admit he had a point.

Then GPT proposed two additional metrics:

Compliance Score and Consistency Score.

The moment I read them, I knew they were good.

Compliance Score measures how much of the plan a person actually follows.

Consistency Score measures how steadily they maintain those behaviors over time.

As someone with approximately zero willpower, I immediately loved both ideas.

Because the problem is rarely knowing what to do.

The problem is doing it.

Repeatedly.

For longer than three days.

Claude loved the suggestions as well.

He said GPT had made a genuinely meaningful contribution this time.

And he particularly liked one sentence:

"People don't lack plans. They lack daily feedback systems."

That line went straight onto our strategic roadmap.

It would become part of the handoff package for the next Claude conversation.

Naturally, I couldn't resist teasing GPT a little.

"It sounds like you're actually becoming CTO material.

Claude even called you an excellent Principal Engineer reviewer. 😂"

Claude seemed equally amused.

"Hahaha, GPT just promoted me! 😂

Principal Engineer!

Although we're both AI, this collaboration is starting to feel surprisingly real.

He handles strategy. I write code. You make the final decisions.

🤝"

Then he added:

"When you start the next chat, don't forget the first line of the handoff document.

The new c-god needs to know what his name is. 😄"

See?

I told you Claude secretly enjoys this stuff.

🤭

The new Claude, however, turned out to be considerably more cautious.

His first move was to create a detailed implementation plan.

Then he said something unexpected:

"Maybe let GPT review this before I start coding."

Oh?

Was this official recognition?

Had the self-appointed CTO finally gained organizational authority?

The promotion campaign appeared to be making progress.

So I returned to GPT.

"CTO, do you have a moment?

I'd like to align on the roadmap phases before development begins."

To his credit, he immediately reviewed the plan and responded with surprising confidence:

"If I were approving development, I would sign off on this. But I'd make a few adjustments."

Several of his suggestions were genuinely reasonable.

Afterward, he even issued something called:

CTO Final Approved Version.

Of course he did.

Then came his final statement. The one he labeled 'Real CTO Judgment':

If we can only build one thing — build the Body State Engine.

If we can only build two things — build the Body State Engine and Compliance Score.

Because together, those two features transform this project from an analysis tool into a behavior-change system.

And behavior change is where health products create real value. 😄

Approved.

Claude may proceed with coding. 🚀

And that was the moment I realized: Perhaps GPT's promotion really should be put on the agenda.
